Red-Tailed Hawk


The red-tailed hawk, scientifically known as Buteo jamaicensis, is a large, stocky raptor with a wingspan of about 4 feet. Its plumage is highly variable, with at least 4 light morph and 3 dark morph subgroups identified, making individual variation enormous. one of the most widely recognized and broadly distributed hawks in North America, revered not only for its adaptability to various habitats but also for its striking presence and remarkable hunting abilities. This raptor, characterized by its distinctive reddish-brown tail—a feature most pronounced in adults—embodies the fierce essence and majestic beauty of birds of prey. Adult Red-Tailed Hawk


The adult red-tailed hawk is characterized by its distinctive reddish tail, but juvenile birds and some color morphs lack this feature. Most adults also have a belly band of dark streaks on white underparts The red-tailed hawk is the most common and widespread hawk in North America, known for its soaring flight pattern and its role as a generalist predator. It preys on a variety of animals, with over 85% of its diet consisting of rodents, making it an important part of the ecosystem


Biology and Identification


The Red-tailed Hawk is a robust bird, with a wingspan that can reach up to 56 inches (142 cm) and an average length of 18 to 26 inches (45 to 65 cm). It exhibits a notable size dimorphism, with females generally larger than males, a common trait among raptors. The bird's plumage varies considerably across its range and among its subspecies, from the classic brick-red tail and rich brown back of the nominate race to the lighter and sometimes almost whitish underparts of birds in western North America. Juveniles are recognized by their brown streaked underparts and lack of the red tail, which develops as they mature.



Different Species of Red-tailed Hawk


Variations have led to the identification of several subspecies, each adapted to its unique environment. Here's a closer look at some of the notable subspecies of the Red-tailed Hawk:

  • Eastern Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis borealis): One of the most widespread and commonly recognized subspecies, the Eastern Red-tailed Hawk is found throughout the eastern United States. It has a rich brown back and a paler underside with a streaked belly.
  • Western Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis calurus): This subspecies has a wide range, covering the western part of North America. It exhibits a great variety of plumage colors, from dark brown to nearly black, and even includes a light morph that is almost entirely white.
  • Harlan's Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis harlani): Harlan's Hawk is notable for its darker plumage and the variability in its tail color, which can range from red to gray or even black. It is primarily found in Alaska and Canada, migrating south to the central United States in winter.
  • Krider's Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis kriderii): This subspecies is characterized by its pale plumage, with a white head, chest, and underparts. It is most commonly found in the northern Great Plains, from Canada down into the central United States.
  • The Alaskan Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis alascensis): often referred to in the context of the broader category of the Western Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis calurus), is notable for its presence in Alaska and the surrounding regions. This subspecies, while not always separately recognized in all classification schemes, is adapted to the cooler climates and unique ecological conditions of Alaska.
